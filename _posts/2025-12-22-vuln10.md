## Introduction — The goal is not to “find a bug”, but to understand the system

A vulnerability is not just a “bug”.
It is a **weakness** in a system (technical or human) that can be **exploited** to achieve an unauthorized outcome: reading data, modifying behavior, disrupting services, or gaining control.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- A pentester does not hunt for random “things”. They hunt for **assumptions**.
- Every action aims to answer one key question:  
  *“What does this system believe to be true… while it is actually false?”*

✅ **Key Takeaways**
- Vulnerability = exploitable weakness  
- Exploit = a way to leverage that weakness  
- The real job = **reducing uncertainty step by step**

### Reflection Questions (eJPT)
1. Which represents a higher risk: a “critical” vulnerability on an unused server, or a “medium” vulnerability on a business-critical system?
2. When you observe unexpected behavior (e.g., unauthorized access), what should come first: *“How can I reproduce it?”* or *“Why does it work?”*

## 1) Types of Vulnerabilities — Understanding *where* things break

Most vulnerabilities can be grouped into a few categories.  
The objective is not to memorize a list, but to understand **where to look** based on what you observe.

### 1. Operating System Vulnerabilities
These exist within the OS or its components and often lead to **privilege escalation** (from a limited user to an administrator or root).

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- Gaining access to a machine is often only half the journey.
- Real power comes from privileges: *who can do what?*

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- “User → admin/root” is a strong indicator of OS or permission-related weaknesses.

---

### 2. (Mis)Configuration Vulnerabilities
The software itself may not be flawed — it is simply **configured incorrectly**.  
Enterprise examples include: public cloud storage, exposed web directories, overly permissive access rights, or unnecessary Internet-facing services.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- Many real-world breaches come from configuration mistakes, not advanced exploits.

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- Misconfigurations are often **easy to exploit**, **common**, and **high-impact**.

---

### 3. Weak or Default Credentials
Systems deployed with default or weak credentials are effectively left **unlocked**.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- When authentication exists, always ask:  
  *“Who manages credentials, and how strong are they really?”*

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- Security does not depend on the presence of a login page, but on **how credentials are managed**.

---

### 4. Application Logic Vulnerabilities
Here, the issue lies in **design**, not implementation.  
Examples include: trusted cookies without verification, bypassable workflows, or missing access control checks.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- You are testing a promise:  
  *“Does the application actually enforce its own rules?”*

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- Logic flaws often evade scanners and require **human reasoning** to identify.

---

### 5. Human-Factor Vulnerabilities
Phishing, social engineering, weak habits, and user mistakes remain some of the most effective attack vectors.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- Cybersecurity protects systems… operated by humans.

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- The “human factor” is not an insult — it is a reality that must be included in risk models.

---

### Reflection Questions (eJPT)
1. If a user can access another user’s data, which category do you suspect first: misconfiguration, OS, application logic, or credentials?
2. In a corporate environment, which is more common: a rare zero-day bug or a configuration mistake? Why?

## 2) CVSS vs VPR — Priority is not the same as severity

A vulnerability can be “severe” on paper yet irrelevant in a given context.  
This is why **prioritization** matters.

### CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System)
CVSS assigns a severity score based on technical criteria such as exploitability and impact on the CIA triad.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- CVSS answers: *“How severe is this vulnerability in general?”*
- But “in general” does not always mean “for this organization”.

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- CVSS is excellent for standardization, weaker for real-world prioritization.

---

### VPR (Vulnerability Priority Rating)
VPR focuses on **actual risk**, incorporating dynamic factors such as threat trends, exploit availability, and organizational relevance.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- VPR answers: *“How risky is this vulnerability right now for this organization?”*

✅ **Key Takeaway**
- VPR is context-aware and more aligned with operational reality.

---

### The Common Trap
- CVSS scores often remain static.
- New exploits, active campaigns, or changes in asset criticality can drastically shift real-world priorities.

🧠 **Pentester Mindset**
- True prioritization comes from combining:  
  **severity × exposure × business value × likelihood of exploitation**

---

### Reflection Questions (eJPT)
1. Is a CVSS “High” vulnerability on a non-exposed system more dangerous than a CVSS “Medium” vulnerability on a public-facing critical application? Explain your reasoning.
2. Why might an organization favor a risk-based scoring model over a purely technical one?
